Throughout Ghana, vehicle owners will decorate their vehicles with statements to be read by those who are stuck in traffic with them. These stickers, similar to US bumper stickers in some ways, are an opportunity to present an idea or theme that is meaningful to the owner. You could learn a bit about what Ghanaians find inspiring or valuable by collecting these sayings.

Over two visits to Ghana in 2025, I made a point of collecting samples of these sayings. The first group, taken in July, was in the greater Kumasi area where my wife and I were hosting two young people who were considering future medical mission service. We were in traffic for over 30 minutes each way from the Freeman Center where we ate and slept and the Ankaase Hospital where we spent each day. Then, in December, we were escorting another mission team to Bolgatanga, and while the trip was not as long, I was still able to collect some sentiments expressed on vehicles, in this case, mostly tricycles.

See this page for photos of vehicle types commonly seen in Ghana and two example sentiments captured by Wikipedia volunteers on those vehicles.

For the July set, I attempted to group the sentiments, starting with the meaning of the Adinkra symbol ‘Gye Nyame’ and how it was quite common. As you mouse over each sentiment, you will see a picture or a link to a resource or two on the web that illustrates the sentiment, or at least may have inspired that phrase. In some cases, I really had no idea! Enjoy exploring!

Diseye Tantua is best known for his iconic style of “Afro Pop Art,” combining graphic symbols, bold colors and expressive text. His drawings are based on traditional proverbs that are made popular by headboards and bumper stickers on buses, taxis, tricycles and street signs.
